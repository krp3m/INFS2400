Cause, Effect, and Solution: Brazil Education

CAUSE: Poverty

• According to UNICEF, 63% of children and adolescents live in multidimensional poverty– including things like income, food, education, housing, water, and sanitation.

EFFECT:

• Students from poorer households attend lower-quality schools and have fewer educational supports.

• Large gaps in reading and learning between high-income vs low-income students: for every 3 high-income students who understand reading, only 1 low-income student does (UPI, 2025).

• Poverty reduces access to study materials, a quiet home environment, and increases risk of child labor or school dropout.

SOLUTION:

• Conditional cash-transfer programs such as Bolsa Família and Fome Zero provide financial support to vulnerable families, helping children remain in school and access nutrition.

• Legal documents enforcing the right to education (Brazil’s 1988 Constitution) ensure access, particularly for low-income families.

CAUSE: Food Insecurity

• A study found that among Brazil’s youth (ages 10-17), about 22 % of those in the lowest socioeconomic strata consume less than 1,200 calories daily, with more than half reporting food-related concerns due to insufficient financial resources (IMDS).

• Poorer families may spend a larger share of their budget on food but still may have inadequate nutritional intake.

EFFECT:

• Poor nutrition impairs cognitive development, concentration, school attendance, and readiness to learn.

• Dependence on school meals means disruptions disproportionately affect food-insecure children.

• Studies show that children in food-insecure households consume fewer nutritious foods and more ultra-processed foods, which negatively impacts learning outcomes (Cambridge, 2019).

SOLUTION:

• School feeding programs improve diet quality and learning outcomes for vulnerable children (Cambridge, 2019).

• Integrated anti-hunger programs like Fome Zero reduce food insecurity while supporting school attendance.

CAUSE: Adult Illiteracy

• Many adults in Brazil cannot read or write at a functional level, which limits opportunities and prevents them from fully supporting their children’s education. Adult illiteracy perpetuates intergenerational cycles of poverty and limits national human capital development (UNESCO, 2022).

EFFECT:

• Adults lacking literacy skills struggle to access better-paying jobs, perpetuating cycles of poverty.

• Children of illiterate adults often have lower educational outcomes due to limited support at home (World Bank, 2021).

• Low literacy in adults contributes to regional disparities; for example, illiteracy is higher in the North-East (~26%) than in the South (~7.7%) (IBGE, 2022).

SOLUTION:

• Alfabetização Solidária (AlfaSol): National program targeting adult literacy, reaching millions of learners in rural and urban poor communities.

• Local education reforms (Sobral/Ceará) emphasize adult literacy alongside early childhood education to break intergenerational illiteracy (World Bank, 2021).
